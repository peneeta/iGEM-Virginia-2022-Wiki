AtheroSHuffle

A lateral flow test strip for proactive atherosclerosis detection.

A Worldwide Health Crisis

Heart disease is the leading cause of death in the United States and around the world.

Each year, one in four Americans die of various cardiovascular complications. [1]

Atherosclerosis is a form of heart disease characterized by plaque-buildup in the inner arterial wall.

It progresses through multiple stages, and is often caught too late.

When plaque begins to form, oxidized low density lipoprotein (OxLDL) concentration increases in the blood, contributing to more plaque formation.

Research suggests that the disease begins developing when people are in their 20s, but treatment does not begin until ages 50-75. [6]

As plaque buildup grows,

blood flow through affected arteries is constricted. [3]

Blood vessels begin to narrow and blood flow decreases throughout the body. This can be fatal, leading to further cardiovascular complications including blood clots, vessel rupture, osteoarthritis, aneurysms, stroke, and heart attack. [4]

Current Treatment Methods are expensive, time-consuming, and invasive.

Currently, physicians use electrocardiograms (EKG) data analysis, CT scans, and angiograms to detect plaque, which can range between $270 to $4700. These methods are expensive and require multiple visits to health professionals to interpret test results.

Diagnostic methods are not included in routine checkups and are not recommended until an individual shows signs of further cardiovascular complications.

To address the need for proactive atherosclerosis testing,

we created AtheroSHuffle

a test strip that uses antibodiees synthesized by the E. coli SHuffle strain to detect OxLDL in blood samples.

How it Works

Our test strip consists of a labelled antibody, an scFv fragment, and a nanobody.
The labelled antibody binds to OxLDL in a blood sample and forms a sandwich complex with the scFv antibody. The nanobody binds to the labelled antibody as a control line.

The Synthetic Biology Puzzle Piece

Typically, these antibodies would be produced by inoculating animals and pulling blood or spleen samples from them, which is harmful and dangerous to them. Each of our antibodies were synthesized using an engineered form of E. coli SHuffle, which has the ability to form disulfide bonds needed for complex protein structures. This provides a safe and humane method of antibody synthesis.
